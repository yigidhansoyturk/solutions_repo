    <section>
        <h2>1. Definition of Cosmic Velocities</h2>
        <p><strong>First Cosmic Velocity:</strong> This is the minimum velocity needed to place an object into orbit around a celestial body, such as a satellite around Earth. For Earth, it's approximately 7.9 km/s.</p>
        <p><strong>Second Cosmic Velocity (Escape Velocity):</strong> This is the speed required to break free from a planet’s gravitational field without further propulsion. For Earth, it's about 11.2 km/s.</p>
        <p><strong>Third Cosmic Velocity:</strong> This is the minimum speed needed to escape the gravitational pull of the Sun and leave the solar system. From Earth, this is around 16.7 km/s.</p>
    </section>

    <section>
        <h2>2. Mathematical Analysis</h2>
        <p>These velocities derive from Newtonian mechanics and the conservation of energy. The general formula for escape velocity is:</p>
        <p><code>v = sqrt(2GM/R)</code></p>
        <p>Where:</p>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>G</strong> is the gravitational constant (6.674×10<sup>−11</sup> m³/kg/s²)</li>
            <li><strong>M</strong> is the mass of the celestial body</li>
            <li><strong>R</strong> is the radius from the center of the body</li>
        </ul>
    </section>

        <h2>4. Importance in Space Exploration</h2>
        <p>Understanding cosmic velocities is fundamental for space missions:</p>
        <ul>
            <li><strong>Satellites:</strong> Launched at first cosmic velocity to maintain orbit.</li>
            <li><strong>Moon/Mars Missions:</strong> Use second cosmic velocity to leave Earth’s gravity.</li>
            <li><strong>Interstellar Probes:</strong> Require third cosmic velocity to leave the solar system, such as the Voyager missions.</li>
